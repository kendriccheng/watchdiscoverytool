# AI Product Builder: Build Project

## Overview

We'd like you to build something. This project is designed to show us how you think about ambiguous problems, how you build against them, and how you communicate what you've done. You have **48 hours** from receiving this brief to push your work to a GitHub repository and share the link with us.

## The Problem

I collect vintage Timex watches and want a better way to stay on top of interesting listings across the market. Today, finding good pieces means manually checking multiple sites, remembering what I like, and hoping I don't miss something.

Build me a tool that:

- Syncs active listings from key marketplaces (eBay, Chrono24, and any others you think are worth including).
- Lets me surface listings that match my preferences:
  - Less than **$50** in total cost (including shipping to M6K1V8)
  - Isn't explicitly broken — I'm willing to replace a battery.
  - Is interesting. Collabs, deadstock, vintage models. Three watches I've bought recently:
    - https://www.ebay.ca/itm/377073705816
    - https://www.ebay.ca/itm/117111976291
    - https://www.etsy.com/ca/listing/4469739360
- Highlights and identifies potential purchase candidates worth paying attention to.

That's the brief. The shape of the solution is yours to decide.

## What to Deliver

1. **A build plan.** Before you start building, write down your plan. What are you building, why, and how? This should live in your repo (a README or separate doc is fine).
2. **A working MVP.** Delivered against that plan. It doesn't need to be production-ready, but it should work and demonstrate real product thinking.
3. **Be ready to walk us through it.** After submission, we'll schedule a session where you present what you built. We want to hear what you built and why — the decisions you made, the tradeoffs you considered, and what you'd do next. We'll ask questions, including about how things work under the hood, so make sure you understand what's in your codebase.

## What We're Evaluating

- Can you take an ambiguous problem and turn it into something concrete and well-scoped?
- Does the thing you built reflect real product thinking — not just code, but decisions about what matters?
- Can you present your work clearly and defend your choices?
- Do you understand what you built — not just what it does, but how it works?

## What This Is Not

This is not a trick question and there's no hidden right answer. We're not looking for the most technically complex solution. We're looking for evidence that you think in products, you can build, and you can communicate. Scope it to something you can do well in the time you have.
